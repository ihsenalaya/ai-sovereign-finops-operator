\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Enterprises are adopting large language models (LLMs) faster than they can govern
them: they cannot say which model truly costs the most, which team consumes what,
when to route to a cheaper model, what a data-sovereignty constraint costs, or
when self-hosting beats a managed API. We present an \emph{economic-aware,
sovereignty-constrained, budget-aware routing control plane} for enterprise LLM
gateways. We formalize model selection as a constrained scoring problem that
rejects any model violating declarative sovereignty rules and, among the rest,
minimizes a weighted combination of cost, expected quality loss, latency, and
budget pressure. We realize the approach as a Kubernetes operator whose FinOps
and sovereignty engines compute auditable decisions and telemetry, and evaluate
it with explicitly separated evidence classes. In the cached experimental
harness, real LLM calls across OpenAI (US, managed) and Mistral (EU, Azure AI
Foundry DataZoneStandard) are replayed deterministically from committed caches.
Relative to an always-premium policy, the governance policy reduces cached
measured cost by \textbf{70.8\%} while keeping LLM-as-judge quality comparable to
premium; a \textbf{30-repetition} uncached statistical run (temperature 0.7)
confirms the cost reduction is highly significant
($p\approx3\times10^{-11}$, Cohen $d\approx-43$) at a significant but bounded
latency increase. In simulated policy scenarios it produces \textbf{zero
sovereignty violations} (vs.\ 40 for a sovereignty-blind baseline) and sustains
100\% request availability with 0\% budget overrun under a tight budget where
hard-blocking drops to 60\%. A bounded live kind verification then shows the
operator scraping real Envoy AI Gateway token telemetry from four apps
(41 input and 897 output tokens, 7655\textmu EUR reported in status) and
detecting real shadow-AI egress through Tetragon. On quality we are deliberately
careful: on
\textbf{500 objective public-benchmark items} (GSM8K + MMLU) our policy
\emph{trades accuracy for cost} (47.2\% vs.\ 65.4\% premium, $-94\%$ cost), and
we show the LLM judge \emph{over-rates incorrect answers} (81.1\% of wrong
answers rated ``acceptable''). We frame the contribution as a \textbf{governance
layer} with a favourable cost/quality trade-off, not a quality-free lunch, and
release all code, datasets, cached responses, and analysis as a reproducible
framework.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LLMs are now embedded in HR assistants, document RAG, developer tooling, and
analytical agents. Each call has a price that depends on the model, the provider,
and the token volume; each call may also send data across jurisdictions. Three
problems recur in regulated enterprises:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Cost opacity.} Teams cannot attribute spend per application/team
        or decide when a cheaper model would suffice.
  \item \textbf{Sovereignty/compliance.} Data may not leave a permitted zone, and
        sensitive prompts may not go to external providers; today this is enforced
        ad hoc, if at all.
  \item \textbf{Managed vs.\ self-hosted arbitrage.} It is unclear at what volume
        self-hosting on GPU becomes cheaper than a managed API.
\end{enumerate}

Existing gateways and FinOps tools mostly \emph{measure and report}. Our thesis is
that a gateway-adjacent control plane can compute and expose auditable routing
decisions: select a model that meets quality and hard sovereignty constraints,
degrade gracefully under budget pressure instead of blocking, attribute spend by
Kubernetes workload, and surface the self-hosting break-even from observed usage.
Full production-grade data-path enforcement, load testing, and failover remain
outside the claims of this paper.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A constrained scoring formulation for LLM routing unifying cost, quality,
        latency, sovereignty, and budget (\S\ref{sec:formulation}).
  \item A Kubernetes/Envoy control-plane realization: the \emph{AI Sovereign
        FinOps Operator}, exposing Cost, Budget, Sovereignty, Break-even, and
        Report engines as CRDs (\S\ref{sec:design}).
  \item A reproducible evaluation methodology and testbed driving the operator's
        actual engines with real LLM telemetry (\S\ref{sec:method}).
  \item A bounded live kind verification that exercises Envoy AI Gateway
        telemetry, Kubernetes attribution, Azure Foundry Cohere/Mistral calls,
        and Tetragon shadow-AI detection without manual seeding.
  \item Empirical evidence across six research questions
        (\S\ref{sec:results}), plus an objective public-benchmark study and an
        LLM-judge validity analysis.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Model routing and cascades.}
A growing line of work trades quality for cost by selecting among models per
request. FrugalGPT~\cite{chen2023frugalgpt} cascades from cheap to expensive
models using a learned scorer; Hybrid LLM~\cite{ding2024hybridllm} routes by
predicted query difficulty with a tunable quality target;
RouteLLM~\cite{ong2024routellm} learns routers from preference data;
AutoMix~\cite{aggarwal2024automix} uses few-shot self-verification and a POMDP
router. These works optimize a \emph{cost--quality} trade-off, largely via
per-request difficulty/confidence prediction for a single tenant. Our work is
\textbf{orthogonal and complementary}: they decide \emph{which model is good
enough}; we add the \textbf{governance layer} around that decision --- hard
data-sovereignty constraints, per-team/namespace budgets, budget-aware graceful
degradation, attribution, and hosting economics --- at the gateway/control-plane
level. Our routing score is intentionally simple (priors, not a learned
difficulty predictor); combining a learned difficulty router with our
sovereignty/budget constraints is explicit future work (\S\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\paragraph{LLM serving systems.}
Orca~\cite{yu2022orca}, vLLM/PagedAttention~\cite{kwon2023pagedattention},
SGLang~\cite{zheng2024sglang}, and DistServe~\cite{zhong2024distserve} optimize
\emph{throughput/latency} of a single self-hosted engine; surveys
\cite{zhou2024surveyinference,li2025tamingtitans} organize this space. They are
complementary: they define the \emph{cost} of the self-hosted option whose
break-even our system predicts (RQ6), but do not address cross-provider routing,
attribution, or sovereignty.

\paragraph{LLM gateways.}
Envoy AI Gateway~\cite{envoyaigateway} and LiteLLM~\cite{litellm} provide a
unified data path to multiple providers and expose usage telemetry, but leave
\emph{policy} (which model, under which constraints, within which budget) to the
operator. We contribute that policy layer as a Kubernetes control plane and
validate it.

\paragraph{Evaluation and regulation.}
We follow the LLM-as-a-judge methodology of Zheng et al.~\cite{zheng2023judge},
which shows strong judges approximate human preference at ${\sim}80\%$ agreement,
adopting both absolute and pairwise protocols while acknowledging its documented
biases (\S\ref{sec:threats}). Data-residency and sensitive-data handling are
driven by the GDPR~\cite{gdpr2016} and the EU AI Act~\cite{euaiact2024}; our
system targets \emph{audit preparation}, not legal attestation.

\section{Problem Formulation and Algorithm}
\label{sec:formulation}
A request $r$ carries metadata (team, namespace, sensitivity) and an estimated
token footprint. Given a catalog of models $M$ (each with provider, zone, managed
flag, per-token price, quality prior, latency prior), a sovereignty policy $P$,
and budget state $(\mathit{used}, \mathit{total})$:

\paragraph{Hard constraint.} Reject any $m$ that violates $P$: its zone is
forbidden; or an allow-list is set and $m$'s zone is outside it (EU covers EU
member states); or $r$ is sensitive, $m$ is a managed external provider, and
external providers are disallowed for sensitive data. Let $C$ be the surviving
candidates. If $C=\varnothing$, the request is not served (a measured outcome,
not a crash).

\paragraph{Soft objective.} Among $C$ meeting a minimum quality $q_{\min}$
(relaxed only when the budget is exhausted, enabling graceful degradation),
select
\begin{equation}
\arg\min_{m\in C}\;
  \alpha\,\widehat{c}(m)\,(1+\varepsilon\rho)
  + \beta\,\ell_q(m)
  + \gamma\,\ell_\lambda(m)
  + \delta\,\sigma(m),
\end{equation}
where $\widehat{c}(m)=\mathrm{cost}(m)/\mathrm{cost}(\mathrm{premium})$ is
normalized cost, $\rho=\mathit{used}/\mathit{total}$ is budget pressure,
$\ell_q(m)=\max\!\big(0,(q_{\mathrm{prem}}-q_m)/q_{\mathrm{prem}}\big)$ is expected
quality loss, $\ell_\lambda$ is normalized latency, and $\sigma$ a residual
sovereignty risk. Budget pressure multiplies the cost term, so as the budget
fills the router shifts to cheaper models. Defaults:
$\alpha{=}1.0,\ \beta{=}1.5,\ \gamma{=}0.3,\ \delta{=}0.5,\ \varepsilon{=}1.0$.
The implementation is \texttt{experimentation/internal/router} (strategy
\texttt{B6-ours}), reusing the operator's sovereignty normalization.

\section{System Design}
\label{sec:design}
Requests flow client $\to$ \textbf{Envoy AI Gateway} $\to$ provider (managed API
or self-hosted). The \textbf{AI Sovereign FinOps Operator} is the control plane:
CRDs declare gateways, providers, models, budgets, and sovereignty policies; pure
engines compute cost (per model/team/namespace), budget phase, sovereignty
findings, and break-even; a report engine emits Markdown/JSON and Prometheus
\texttt{ai\_finops\_*} metrics. The design keeps the data path in Envoy and the
decision logic in the operator. In this paper the routing decision is evaluated
primarily at the control-plane level. The repository also contains a bounded
live kind verification in which Envoy AI Gateway emits real token telemetry and
the operator attributes it per namespace/application. This validates the
telemetry and attribution path, not production-grade load, failover, or legal
compliance.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Evidence labels.} To avoid mixing claims, each result is interpreted
under one of four labels. \textbf{MEASURED} means observed in a live execution or
fresh API run. \textbf{CACHED} means replayed from committed responses produced by
earlier real calls. \textbf{SIMULATED} means generated by deterministic policy or
workload simulation over declared catalogs. \textbf{MODELED} means computed from
an analytical model, such as GPU break-even economics. We do not present modeled
self-hosting economics as GPU/vLLM measurements.

\paragraph{Testbed.} A Go harness drives the operator's \emph{actual} engines and
issues \textbf{real} LLM calls across \textbf{two providers}: (1) \textbf{OpenAI
(US, managed)} --- gpt-4o (premium), gpt-4o-mini (medium), gpt-4.1-nano (cheap);
(2) \textbf{Mistral (EU)} --- \texttt{mistral-large} served via \textbf{Azure AI
Foundry} as \textbf{Mistral-Large-3} in \emph{DataZoneStandard} (EU data zone), a
real EU-hosted provider. A \emph{modeled} EU self-hosted model (cost computed,
response stubbed, rows tagged \emph{modeled}) is retained only for the RQ6
break-even prediction. The harness is provider-agnostic (per-provider
OpenAI-compatible clients), so adding providers is additive.

\paragraph{Workloads (W1--W4).} HR chatbot (short, sensitive), RAG docs (long
context, quality-sensitive), Dev assistant (high quality), Analytical agent (high
volume). Each declares team, namespace, sensitivity, monthly budget, premium
model, allowed models, and $q_{\min}$.

\paragraph{Baselines.} B1 premium-static, B2 round-robin, B3 least-cost, B4
static namespace policy, B5 budget hard-block, B6 ours; plus B7 a
literature-style difficulty router (Hybrid-LLM/RouteLLM proxy).

\paragraph{Metrics.} Cost (total, per request, per token, per team via the
operator cost engine, \% savings); quality (LLM-as-judge acceptability 1--5
$\to$ normalized 0--1, acceptable-rate $\geq3$, pairwise win-rate vs.\ premium);
latency (p50/p95/p99, mean, routing-decision $\mu$s); sovereignty (violations,
reroutes, blocked); budget (availability, overrun); break-even (managed vs.\
self-hosted monthly, payback).

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} Temperature 0 for the deterministic pass; all
responses and judge scores cached so re-runs are identical and free; every test
journaled with status+duration (42 PASS / 0 FAIL). Latency 95\% CIs via
bootstrap. The protocol also supports the $\geq30$-repetition run reported in
\S\ref{sec:stats}.

\paragraph{Bounded live kind verification.} In addition to the harness, we ran a
fresh local kind verification on 2026-06-13 using
\path{make -C automatisation real-demo-verify}. The run rebuilt the operator
image from the local source, deployed Envoy Gateway v1.5.0 and Envoy AI Gateway
v0.7.0, deployed four bounded apps, collected logs/CRDs/metrics/Tetragon
evidence, scaled the apps down, and deleted the kind cluster. Azure was used only
for the model endpoints; no AKS cluster or GPU/vLLM workload was created. The
evidence bundle is \path{experimentation/results-live-kind-20260613-verify-v2}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Live kind verification --- telemetry, attribution, and cleanup
(\textbf{MEASURED})}
The bounded live verification completed successfully on a fresh kind cluster and
then deleted the cluster. All four verification apps reached one real successful
gateway call and then idled: \texttt{rh/chatbot-rh},
\texttt{finance/risk-assistant}, and \texttt{legal/contract-review} called Azure
Foundry Cohere; \texttt{marketing/content-writer} called Azure Foundry Mistral.
Envoy AI Gateway exported per-namespace/application token telemetry:
10/219 input/output tokens for \texttt{rh/chatbot-rh}, 7/319 for
\texttt{finance/risk-assistant}, 10/299 for \texttt{legal/contract-review}, and
14/60 for \texttt{marketing/content-writer}. The operator reported
\texttt{totalInputTokens=41}, \texttt{totalOutputTokens=897}, and
\texttt{totalCostEUR=7655u} in the \texttt{AIFinOpsReport} status after the
sub-cent precision fix. Tetragon exported real process events for
\texttt{finance/rogue-script} connecting directly to \texttt{api.openai.com};
the resulting \texttt{shadow-egress} ConfigMap contained four observed
connections and the sovereignty policy reported seven findings.

This is a bounded integration verification, not a throughput benchmark. Its
purpose is to validate that the Kubernetes operator can consume real Envoy AI
Gateway token telemetry and real Tetragon shadow-AI evidence without manual
seeding, and that the automation stops apps and deletes the cluster after
collecting evidence.

\subsection{RQ1 --- Cost}
In the cached harness (\textbf{CACHED}), relative to premium-static,
\textbf{B6-ours cuts total cost by 70.8\%}
(0.01135 vs.\ 0.03893 EUR over the 40-prompt matrix; cost/request 0.000284 vs.\
0.000973). With the second provider available, Ours routes much traffic to the
cheaper Mistral EU and OpenAI mid/cheap tiers. A \emph{literature-style
difficulty router} (B7) saves slightly \emph{more} (73.1\%) but at lower quality
(0.866) and with no sovereignty/budget/attribution governance. Na\"ive least-cost
(B3) saves 98.2\% at the largest quality cost; round-robin 62.3\%; static policy
46.0\% (Figure~\ref{fig:cost}).

\caption{CACHED: total cost by routing strategy in the harness (lower is
better).}\label{fig:cost}

\subsection{RQ2 --- Quality (LLM-as-judge)}
Within this two-provider, single-pass scope, B6-ours's mean normalized quality is
\textbf{comparable to --- marginally above --- premium} (0.9125 vs.\ 0.900;
acceptable-rate 97.5\%), with a pairwise win-rate vs.\ premium of \textbf{50.0\%}
(statistical parity). The cross-provider mix helps: Mistral-Large-3 sometimes
matches or beats gpt-4o on the judge. Least-cost drops to 0.858, static-policy to
0.869, and the difficulty router (B7) to 0.866. Two caveats: (i) the premium
model is also the routing reference and judge anchor, which can bias pairwise
results; (ii) this is a single deterministic pass. We therefore claim only that
\emph{quality remained comparable within the evaluated scope}
(Figure~\ref{fig:qvc}).

\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{fig3_quality_vs_cost.png}
\caption{CACHED/JUDGE-ASSESSED: quality vs.\ cost in the open-ended harness.
This is a proxy quality signal, not objective correctness.}\label{fig:qvc}
\end{figure}

\subsection{RQ2b --- Objective accuracy + judge validity}
To remove judge bias and test generality, we evaluate on \textbf{500 real
public-benchmark items} --- 250 \textbf{GSM8K} (math, numeric) and 250
\textbf{MMLU} (multiple-choice A--D) --- scored by \textbf{objective exact-match}.
Premium-static (gpt-4o) reaches \textbf{65.4\%} accuracy at 0.116 EUR;
\textbf{Ours 47.2\%} at 0.0073 EUR (\textbf{$-94\%$ cost}); static-policy 59.4\%;
least-cost/difficulty-router 44--46\%. On these objective tasks aggressive cost
routing \emph{trades accuracy for cost}.

\paragraph{Why this contradicts the judge (a methodological finding).}
We re-scored 300 objective answers with \emph{both} exact-match and the LLM judge:
the judge rated \textbf{81.1\% of objectively \emph{incorrect} answers as
``acceptable'' ($\geq3$)}, with nearly identical mean judge scores for correct
(4.49) vs.\ incorrect (4.14) answers and near-zero point-biserial correlation
($r{=}0.12$). \textbf{The LLM-as-judge over-estimates quality on objective tasks}
--- it cannot distinguish fluent-but-wrong from correct
(Figure~\ref{fig:judge}). This explains the RQ2-vs-RQ2b gap, and we adopt the
consequence throughout: report judge-based quality only for open-ended workloads,
exact-match for tasks with ground truth, and never claim quality preservation
from the judge alone.

\begin{figure}[H]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{fig_judge_vs_truth.png}
\caption{LLM-judge score by objective correctness: the judge over-rates wrong
answers.}\label{fig:judge}
\end{figure}

\subsection{RQ3 --- Latency and overhead}
The \textbf{routing decision adds tens of microseconds} (B6 ${\approx}26.5\,\mu$s/
request) --- negligible vs.\ network latency. End-to-end latency tracks the chosen
model and provider-side variance. Notably, B6's p95 (3475 ms) exceeds
premium-static's (2494 ms), which we do not hide: B6 mixes models, giving a
heavier-tailed latency distribution. We resolve whether this is real or
single-pass noise in \S\ref{sec:stats}. Least-cost has the lowest latency
(p95 1476 ms), consistent with always picking small models
(Figure~\ref{fig:lat}).

\begin{figure}[H]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{fig4_latency.png}
\caption{CACHED/MEASURED LATENCY: latency percentiles by strategy in the harness.}\label{fig:lat}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Gateway data-path overhead (MEASURED, standalone Envoy).}
Beyond control-plane decision time, we measured routing through a real
\textbf{Envoy} data path: an Envoy proxy in front of the provider, driven at
concurrency 8. Envoy adds \textbf{negligible overhead} --- mean latency 1153 ms
vs.\ 1116 ms direct (${\approx}{+}3\%$, within run-to-run variance), throughput
6.05 vs.\ 5.81 req/s, \textbf{0 errors} --- supporting the feasibility of a
gateway-level control plane. The bounded kind verification above validates
in-cluster Envoy AI Gateway telemetry and attribution; production-grade
load/failover testing remains future work.

\subsection{RQ4 --- Sovereignty (\textbf{SIMULATED policy scenarios over real/catalogued providers})}
A sovereignty-blind baseline (B1) commits \textbf{40 violations} under eu-only,
france-only and self-hosted-only, and 10 under no-external-sensitive.
\textbf{B6-ours commits zero violations in every scenario.} Crucially, with a
\textbf{real EU provider} (Mistral-Large-3, Azure AI Foundry DataZone EU), under
the \textbf{EU-only} policy Ours \textbf{keeps 100\% availability (40/40 served)
with zero violations} by rerouting to Mistral EU (quality 0.866) --- where the
blind baseline would commit 40 violations. The \emph{cost of sovereignty} appears
in the stricter france-only/self-hosted-only scenarios: only the modeled FR
self-hosted model qualifies, so 20/40 are served and the rest refused rather than
violated --- quantifying the availability price of strict residency
(Figure~\ref{fig:sov}).

\subsection{Ablation}
Removing the \textbf{cost term} reverts spend to premium (0.0389 EUR) at
unchanged quality --- the cost term produces the saving. Removing the
\textbf{quality term} drives spend to the floor (0.0012 EUR) but lowers quality
to 0.863 --- the quality term protects it. The latency term has negligible effect
here. The full system attains the cost saving \emph{and} the quality floor
(Figure~\ref{fig:abl}).

\subsection{Statistical significance (N=30 repetitions, \textbf{MEASURED fresh API calls})}
\label{sec:stats}
To move beyond the single deterministic pass, we re-ran the full matrix
\textbf{30 times} at temperature 0.7 with caches bypassed (independent samples;
judge $=$ gpt-4o-mini to bound cost), and tested each strategy against
premium-static on the per-repetition distributions (Mann-Whitney U, Cliff's
$\delta$, Cohen's $d$, 95\% CIs).
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cost.} Ours 0.0110 EUR/rep [0.0109, 0.0110] vs.\ premium 0.0385
        [0.0382, 0.0389] --- a \textbf{71.4\% reduction},
        $p\approx3\times10^{-11}$, Cliff $\delta=-1.00$, Cohen $d=-43$
        (non-overlapping CIs): highly significant with a maximal effect size.
  \item \textbf{Quality.} Ours mean normalized quality 0.9587 [0.9564, 0.9611]
        vs.\ premium 0.9362 --- under this judge Ours is significantly higher
        ($p\approx6\times10^{-11}$, $\delta=+0.97$, $d=+3.4$), while na\"ive
        least-cost is significantly lower (0.869, $d=-13$). The \emph{sign} of the
        small quality gap depends on the judge (the single-pass gpt-4o judge
        showed parity), so we claim quality is \emph{at least comparable}.
  \item \textbf{Latency.} Ours is significantly higher (1506 ms/rep [1459, 1553]
        vs.\ 998, $p\approx5\times10^{-10}$, $d=+3.7$) --- confirming the
        routing-mix latency cost is \emph{real, not single-pass noise}. Least-cost
        is lowest (707 ms); the latency/cost trade-off is now quantified.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
Economic-aware routing is most valuable when a workload mixes easy and hard
requests: the router sends cheap requests to cheap models and reserves the premium
model for quality-critical ones, capturing most of the savings at little quality
cost. Strict sovereignty has a real availability/quality price that the system
makes explicit and auditable rather than hidden. For budget control, graceful
degradation strictly dominates hard-blocking on availability and overrun.
Self-hosting pays off only at high, sustained volume.

Table~\ref{tab:recap} consolidates every research question into a single
gains/losses ledger versus the always-premium baseline, so the trade-off is
visible at a glance: large, statistically significant cost and governance gains,
bought with a real latency increase and --- on objective tasks --- a real
accuracy loss.

\subsection{When \emph{not} to use economic routing}
Economic routing is not a universal win; our own results delimit where to avoid it:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{High-stakes, correctness-critical tasks.} On objective reasoning
        (RQ2b) cheaper models genuinely fail, so cost routing trades accuracy for
        cost (47.2\% vs.\ 65.4\%). Where a wrong answer is expensive (legal,
        medical, shipping code, financial calculations), pin the premium model.
  \item \textbf{Low volume / low spend.} Below a few hundred thousand tokens/month
        the absolute saving is small and does not justify the added control-plane
        complexity; premium-static is simpler.
  \item \textbf{Strict tail-latency SLOs.} Ours shows a significant tail-latency
        increase (RQ3, $d{=}+3.7$) from mixing models; for ultra-low-latency
        paths, a single fast model is preferable.
  \item \textbf{No reliable quality signal.} Our priors miss task-specific
        difficulty and the LLM judge over-rates wrong answers (RQ2b); without a
        trustworthy per-task quality estimate, routing can silently degrade
        quality --- prefer a fixed model or add a calibrated/learned difficulty
        predictor first.
  \item \textbf{No governance need.} If there is no sovereignty, budget, or
        attribution requirement (single tenant, single jurisdiction, flat
        budget), the governance layer adds little; a plain cost--quality router
        (or none) suffices.
  \item \textbf{Homogeneous high-quality workloads.} If essentially every request
        needs the premium model, there is nothing to route --- the policy
        degenerates to premium-static.
\end{itemize}
In short, our approach targets \textbf{multi-tenant, governance-constrained,
mixed-difficulty, sufficiently high-volume} settings; outside that envelope its
benefits shrink or invert.

\section{Planned Study: Human FinOps Expert vs.\ Operator (RQ7)}
\label{sec:rq7}
The evaluation so far compares the operator against \emph{algorithmic} baselines.
A complementary question --- reserved for a future version of this work and stated
here for transparency --- is how close the control plane's decisions are to those
of \emph{experienced human practitioners}. We have \textbf{not} run this study and
report no results; we publish the protocol so the claim space is explicit and the
study is pre-registered.

\paragraph{Research question.} How closely do the control plane's routing and
managed-vs-self-host recommendations match the decisions of experienced
FinOps/cloud experts, and where do they diverge?

\paragraph{Design.} $N\geq6$--$10$ practitioners (cloud/FinOps/platform engineers
with LLM-ops experience) each work through a set of decision scenarios drawn from
the testbed. Every scenario provides a workload profile, the model/provider
catalog with prices, the sovereignty policy, the budget state, and recent usage
telemetry. For each scenario the expert chooses (a) which model/provider to route
to and (b) the managed-vs-self-host recommendation, with a short written
rationale. The operator is run on the \emph{same} scenarios to produce the same
two decisions.

\paragraph{Metrics.} (i) \textbf{Agreement} between expert and system: exact-match
rate on the model/provider choice and on the host/keep-managed recommendation,
summarized with Cohen's $\kappa$ across experts and against the system; (ii)
\textbf{decision time}, human vs.\ automated; (iii) \textbf{rationale overlap} ---
which signals experts cite (cost, sovereignty, budget, quality) vs.\ the system's
stated reasons; (iv) a qualitative \textbf{disagreement analysis} of where and why
the two diverge.

\paragraph{Threats and honesty.} The expert pool is small, scenario realism is
imperfect, and experts may lack full price visibility; we mitigate with diverse
recruitment, realistic telemetry, and pre-registration of the analysis. Until the
study is run, no agreement scores or expert decisions are reported --- RQ7 remains
explicitly future work (protocol: \texttt{RQ7\_HUMAN\_EXPERT\_PROTOCOL.md}).

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}
\textbf{Internal:} API/latency variability (mitigated by temperature 0, caching,
bootstrap CIs); LLM-as-judge bias (absolute + pairwise, fixed judge).
\textbf{External:} two providers and \emph{modeled} self-hosting in this phase;
four synthetic workloads; modeled GPU economics (RQ6); the live kind verification
is bounded and local, not an AKS or production load test; the Mistral EU model
uses Azure AI Foundry DataZoneStandard (EU data zone) --- strict France-only
residency still relies on a modeled fallback; volatile cloud prices are isolated
as configuration. \textbf{Construct:} acceptability/win-rate are quality proxies;
declarative sovereignty simplifies legal reality --- the system enforces declared
technical routing policies and supports audit preparation, but does not
independently certify legal compliance or provider data residency claims.
\textbf{System validity:} the live verification proves telemetry scrape,
namespace/application attribution, and Tetragon shadow-AI ingestion under a small
kind workload; it does not prove production-grade Envoy AI Gateway enforcement,
failover, or scale. \textbf{Conclusion:} beyond the
deterministic pass we report a 30-repetition run (temperature 0.7) with 95\% CIs,
Mann-Whitney U significance and Cliff's $\delta$/Cohen's $d$ effect sizes; the
cost reduction and the latency increase are highly significant with large effect
sizes. The remaining caveat is \emph{judge dependence} of the small quality gap. A
two-judge agreement study (gpt-4o vs.\ Mistral-Large, $n{=}120$) finds
\emph{moderate} agreement --- Cohen's quadratic-weighted $\kappa=0.40$,
Krippendorff $\alpha=0.39$, Spearman $\rho=0.61$ ($p\approx10^{-13}$), within-1
agreement 94.2\% --- which is precisely why we treat the small quality gap as
judge-dependent and plan a human evaluation (a blind 100-item human-eval package
is prepared and awaits evaluators).

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Within our preliminary, two-provider testbed, a gateway-level, economic-aware,
sovereignty-constrained, budget-aware control plane substantially reduced cached
harness cost ($-70.8\%$) while keeping open-ended judge quality comparable, and
the simulated policy scenarios showed zero violations under declared provider
metadata and better availability under budget pressure. A bounded live kind run
then verified the engineering path from Envoy AI Gateway token telemetry and
Tetragon shadow-AI events into operator status and metrics, with automatic
cleanup. We deliberately frame these as \emph{preliminary evidence and a
reproducible framework}, not universal or production-grade claims. To reach a
defensible Q1 result we plan: (1) more providers; (2) real GPU self-hosting to
validate the modeled break-even; (3) multi-judge + human quality evaluation; (4)
human evaluation to settle the judge-dependent quality sign (the 100-item package
is ready; the N=30 statistics and the judge-vs-ground-truth study are done); (5)
production-grade Envoy AI Gateway enforcement with load testing and failover; (6)
a human FinOps expert vs.\ automated control-plane study; and (7) integrating a
learned difficulty router with our governance constraints. The paper claims only
what the committed results support.

\paragraph{Artifact / Reproducibility.} The operator and \path{experimentation/}
(datasets, harness, scripts, results, figures, cached responses, and live kind
evidence bundles) constitute the reproducible artifact. Reproduce harness results
with \path{scripts/run_experiment.sh} then
\path{python3 scripts/analyze_results.py}; reproduce the bounded live
verification with \path{make -C automatisation real-demo-verify}. Every reported
test is journaled; failed or negative runs are preserved rather than relabeled.
