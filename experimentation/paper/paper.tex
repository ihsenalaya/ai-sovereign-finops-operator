\begin{abstract}
Enterprises adopt large language models (LLMs) faster than they can govern them: they cannot say which
model truly costs the most, which team consumes what, what a data-sovereignty constraint costs, or when
self-hosting beats a managed API. We present an \emph{economic-aware, sovereignty-constrained,
budget-aware routing control plane} for enterprise LLM gateways, realised as a Kubernetes operator whose
FinOps and sovereignty engines drive routing. We evaluate it with real LLM calls across two providers
(OpenAI US + Mistral EU via Azure AI Foundry, EU data zone) over four synthetic enterprise workloads and
500 public-benchmark items (GSM8K, MMLU). Relative to always-premium routing, our approach reduces
measured cost by $\sim$71\% (30-repetition run: $p\approx3\times10^{-11}$, Cohen $d\approx-43$) at a
significant but bounded latency increase, enforces five declarative sovereignty scenarios with zero
violations (vs.\ 40 for a sovereignty-blind baseline) and keeps 100\% availability under an EU-only
policy via a real EU provider. On quality we are deliberately careful: an LLM judge finds our routing
comparable on open-ended workloads, but on objective benchmarks our policy trades accuracy for cost
(47.2\% vs.\ 65.4\%, $-94\%$ cost), and we show the LLM judge over-rates incorrect answers (81\% of
wrong answers rated ``acceptable''). We frame the contribution as a governance layer with a favourable
cost/quality trade-off, not a quality-free lunch, and release all code, data and analysis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LLMs are embedded in HR assistants, document RAG, developer tooling and analytical agents. Each call has
a provider- and token-dependent price and may cross jurisdictions. Three problems recur in regulated
enterprises: \textbf{cost opacity}, \textbf{sovereignty/compliance}, and \textbf{managed-vs-self-hosted
arbitrage}. Existing gateways and FinOps tools mostly \emph{measure and report}; our thesis is that a
control plane at the gateway can \emph{act}: route each request to the cheapest model meeting a quality
bar and all hard sovereignty constraints, degrade gracefully under budget pressure rather than block,
and surface the self-hosting break-even from observed usage.
\textbf{Contributions:} (i) a constrained scoring formulation unifying cost, quality, latency,
sovereignty and budget; (ii) a Kubernetes/Envoy control-plane realisation (the AI Sovereign FinOps
Operator) with Cost, Budget, Sovereignty, Break-even and Report engines as CRDs; (iii) a reproducible
evaluation methodology; (iv) empirical evidence across six research questions plus an objective
public-benchmark study and an LLM-judge validity analysis.

\section{Related Work and Positioning}
Model routing/cascades trade quality for cost via per-request difficulty prediction: FrugalGPT
\cite{chen2023frugalgpt}, Hybrid LLM \cite{ding2024hybridllm}, RouteLLM \cite{ong2024routellm}, AutoMix
\cite{aggarwal2024automix}. LLM serving systems optimise throughput/latency of a single engine: Orca
\cite{yu2022orca}, vLLM \cite{kwon2023pagedattention}, SGLang \cite{zheng2024sglang}, DistServe
\cite{zhong2024distserve}; surveys \cite{zhou2024surveyinference,li2025tamingtitans}. Gateways
(Envoy AI Gateway \cite{envoyaigateway}, LiteLLM \cite{litellm}) expose telemetry but leave policy to the
operator. Evaluation follows LLM-as-judge \cite{zheng2023judge}; regulation follows GDPR \cite{gdpr2016}
and the EU AI Act \cite{euaiact2024}. Our work is \emph{orthogonal}: prior routers decide \emph{which
model is good enough}; we add the governance layer (hard sovereignty, per-tenant budgets, graceful
degradation, attribution, hosting economics) at the control-plane level (Table~\ref{tab:matrix}).

\section{Problem Formulation and Algorithm}
Given a request $r$ (team, namespace, sensitivity, token estimate), a model catalog $M$ (provider, zone,
managed flag, per-token price, quality/latency priors), a sovereignty policy $P$ and budget state
$(\mathit{used},\mathit{total})$: reject any $m$ violating $P$ (forbidden zone; outside an allow-list
where EU covers EU members; sensitive request to a managed external provider when disallowed). Among the
compliant candidates $C$ meeting minimum quality $q_{\min}$ (relaxed only when the budget is exhausted,
enabling graceful degradation), select
\begin{equation}
\arg\min_{m\in C}\ \alpha\,\widehat{c}(m)(1+\varepsilon\rho) + \beta\,\ell_q(m) + \gamma\,\ell_\lambda(m) + \delta\,\sigma(m),
\end{equation}
with normalized cost $\widehat{c}$, budget pressure $\rho=\mathit{used}/\mathit{total}$, quality loss
$\ell_q=\max(0,(q_{\text{prem}}-q_m)/q_{\text{prem}})$, latency penalty $\ell_\lambda$, sovereignty risk
$\sigma$; defaults $\alpha{=}1,\beta{=}1.5,\gamma{=}0.3,\delta{=}0.5,\varepsilon{=}1$.

\section{Methodology}
A Go harness drives the operator's actual engines and issues real calls to two providers (OpenAI US;
Mistral EU = Mistral-Large-3 on Azure AI Foundry, DataZoneStandard). Four enterprise workloads and 500
public-benchmark items (250 GSM8K, 250 MMLU). Baselines B1--B5 (premium, round-robin, least-cost,
static, hard-block), B7 (difficulty router, Hybrid/RouteLLM-style proxy) and B6 (ours). Metrics: cost
(operator cost engine), judge quality (open-ended) and exact-match accuracy (ground truth), latency,
sovereignty, budget. Determinism via temperature 0; a 30-repetition run at temperature 0.7 gives
distributions; responses cached for reproducibility; every test is journaled (no skips).

\section{Results}
\textbf{RQ1 Cost.} Ours $-70.8\%$ vs.\ premium; B7 $-73\%$ at lower quality; least-cost $-98\%$ at the
largest quality cost. \textbf{RQ2 Quality (judge).} Ours comparable on open-ended workloads (judge).
\textbf{RQ2b Objective + judge validity.} On 500 GSM8K/MMLU items premium reaches 65.4\% vs.\ Ours 47.2\%
($-94\%$ cost); the LLM judge rated 81.1\% of \emph{wrong} answers ``acceptable'' (point-biserial
$r{=}0.12$), so judge-quality over-estimates correctness (Fig.~\ref{fig:judge}). \textbf{RQ3 Latency.}
Routing overhead $\sim$26\,$\mu$s; end-to-end latency significantly higher for Ours (real trade-off,
$d{=}+3.7$). \textbf{RQ4 Sovereignty.} Ours 0 violations across five scenarios; under EU-only it keeps
100\% availability via the real EU provider where the blind baseline commits 40 violations. \textbf{RQ5
Budget.} Graceful degradation: 100\% availability, 0\% overrun vs.\ hard-block 60\%. \textbf{RQ6
Break-even.} Managed-vs-self-hosted crossover from telemetry (modeled). \textbf{Statistics (N=30):} cost
reduction $p\approx3\times10^{-11}$, Cohen $d\approx-43$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:stats}).

\section{Threats to Validity}
LLM-API variability (temperature 0, caching, bootstrap CIs); LLM-judge over-rates objective answers
(hence exact-match where ground truth exists); modeled self-hosting economics; two providers; synthetic
enterprise workloads; declarative sovereignty simplifies legal reality (we target audit preparation, not
attestation). A blind human-evaluation package (100 items) is prepared and awaits evaluators.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
A gateway control plane can substantially cut LLM cost under governance constraints with a favourable
(not free) quality trade-off, real EU-sovereignty enforcement and graceful budget degradation. Future
work: human evaluation; live Envoy data-path enforcement under load; grafting a learned difficulty
router; more providers; and validating the modeled break-even.
